The European Space Agency&apos;s Jupiter Icy Moon Explorer is a mission for Europe (and for neighboring Ganymede and I), which is scheduled to launch in 2022. ENG=Saturn is the sixth planet in the solar system in order of distance from the Sun and the second most massive planet after Jupiter. With an average radius 9.5 times that of the Earth and a mass 95 times greater than that of Earth, Saturn, with Jupiter, Uranus and Neptune, is classified as a gas giant. The name derives from the homonymous god of Roman mythology, counterpart of the Greek titan Cronus. Its astronomical symbol is a stylized representation of the sickle of the god of agriculture and the passage of time. Saturn is composed of 95% hydrogen and 3% helium followed by the other elements. The core, consisting of silicates and ice, is surrounded by a thick layer of metallic hydrogen and therefore by an external gaseous layer. The winds in Saturn&apos;s atmosphere can reach 1 800 km / h, resulting significantly faster than those on Jupiter and slightly less fast than those blowing in the atmosphere of Neptune. Saturn has an extensive and conspicuous ring system consisting mainly of ice particles and silicate dust. Of the sixty known moons that orbit the planet, Titan is the largest and also the only moon in the solar system to have a significant atmosphere.

ENG=Mars is the fourth planet in the solar system in order of distance from the Sun; it is visible to the naked eye and is the last of the terrestrial planets after Mercury, Venus and Earth. Called the red planet because of its characteristic color due to the large quantities of iron oxide that cover it. Mars takes its name from the homonymous divinity of Roman mythology and its astronomical symbol is the stylized representation of the shield and spear of the god.  Pur presenting rather low average surface temperatures (between -120 &deg; C and -14 &deg; C) and a very rarefied atmosphere, it is the planet most similar to Earth among those of the solar system. Its dimensions are intermediate between those of our planet and of the Moon, and it presents an inclination of the axis of rotation and duration of the day similar to the terrestrial ones. Its surface has volcanic formations, valleys, polar caps and sandy deserts, and geological formations that suggest the presence of a hydrosphere in the distant past. The surface of the planet appears strongly cratered, due to the almost total absence of erosive agents (geological, atmospheric and hydrosphere activity, the main ones) and the total absence of plate tectonic activity capable of forming and then modeling the tectonic structures. The very low density of the atmosphere is then unable to consume a large part of the meteorites, which therefore reach the ground more frequently than on Earth. Among the most noteworthy geological formations on Mars, Olympus Mons or Mount Olympus, the largest volcano in the solar system (27 meters high); the Valles Marineris, a long canyon considerably larger than the terrestrial ones and a huge crater on the boreal hemisphere about 40% of the entire Martian surface.  At direct observation Mars presents color variations , historically attributed to the presence of seasonal vegetation, which change as the periods of the year change. Subsequent spectroscopic observations of the atmosphere have long since led to the hypothesis that there could be seas, canals and rivers or a sufficiently dense atmosphere. The final denial came from the Mariner 4 mission, which in 1965 showed a desert and arid planet, characterized by periodic and particularly violent sandstorms. More recent missions have highlighted the presence of water in the form of ice. Around the planet orbit two natural satellites, Fobos and Deimos, small in size and irregular in shape.

ENG=Uranus is the seventh planet in the solar system in order of distance from the Sun, third in diameter and fourth in mass. Its astronomical Unicode symbol is, stylization of the initial letter H by William Herschel). It bears the name of the Greek god of the sky Uranus, father of Cronus (Saturn), in turn father of Zeus (Jupiter). Although it is also visible to the naked eye, like the other five planets known since antiquity, it was never recognized as such due to its low brightness and its particularly slow orbit and was identified as something other than a star only the March 13, 1781 by William Herschel. A curiosity about his discovery is that it came completely unexpected: the planets visible to the naked eye (up to Saturn) had been known for millennia and no one suspected the existence of other planets, until the discovery of Herschel which he noticed as a particular starlet seemed to move. From that moment on, no one was more sure of the real number of planets in our solar system. The chemical composition of Uranus is similar to that of Neptune and both have a different composition than that of the larger gas giants (Jupiter and Saturn). For this reason, astronomers sometimes prefer to refer to these two planets by treating them as a separate class, the &quot;frozen giants&quot;. The atmosphere of the planet, although similar to that of Jupiter and Saturn due to the abundant presence of hydrogen and helium, contains a high proportion of &quot;ice&quot;, such as water, ammonia and methane, along with traces of hydrocarbons. Uranus is also the coldest atmosphere in the solar system, with a minimum temperature that can drop to 49 (-224 &deg; C). It has a complex well-layered cloud structure, in which the water is thought to be in the lower layers and methane in the higher ones. On the contrary, the interior of the planet would be composed mainly of ice and rocks. One of the most unusual characteristics of the planet is the orientation of its rotation axis. All the other planets have their own axis almost perpendicular to the plane of the orbit, while that of Uranus is almost parallel. It then rotates by exposing one of its poles to the Sun for half the revolution period with consequent extremes of the seasonal phases. Furthermore, since the axis is inclined by a little more than 90 &deg;, the rotation is technically retrograde: Uranus rotates in the opposite direction to that of all the other planets of the solar system (except Venus) even if, given the exceptionality of the inclination the retrograde rotation is only a minor note. The period of its revolution around the Sun is about 84 Earth years. The orbit of Uranus deviates very little from the ecliptic (inclination of 0.7 &deg;). Like the other giant planets, Uranus has a system of planetary rings, a magnetosphere and numerous satellites; seen from Earth, due to the inclination of the planet, its rings can sometimes appear as a concentric system that surrounds the planet, or as in 2007 and 2008 appear cut. In 1986 the Voyager 2 spacecraft showed Uranus as a planet with no distinctive mark on its surface, without the bands and storms typical of other gas giants. However, later observations from Earth showed evidence of changes related to the seasons and an increase in climatic activity when the planet approached the equinox.
